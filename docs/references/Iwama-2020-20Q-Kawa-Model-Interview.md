## Key Concepts for Hornelore

### The Four Elements (cross-section of the river)

| Element | Metaphor | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| **Water** | Flow/current | Life flow and health — quality of everyday living |
| **Rocks** | Obstacles in riverbed | Life circumstances and problems — injuries, illness, disruptions |
| **River walls/sides/bottom** | Channel shape | Environmental factors — social and physical environment |
| **Driftwood** | Floating debris | Personal assets AND liabilities — traits, skills, education, personality |

All four are **inseparably connected** and always in flux. If one changes, all others change.

### Driftwood Is Dual-Valence

Iwama is explicit: the same personal trait can be both asset and liability.

> "Your stubbornness and your OCD tendencies may become a point of frustration if you incur an injury... At the same time, those same traits can become an asset if you are organized and determined."

Hornelore implication: Kawa segment schema should allow driftwood items to carry both positive and negative valence, or shift valence across life stages.

### Cross-Sections = River Segments

Iwama describes taking a cross-section at any point along the river's length:

> "If you were to take a cross-section of this river at any point along its length, you would see essentially four elements that are always in interplay with one another."

This maps directly to Hornelore's Kawa segment model: each segment is a cross-section at a life stage.

### The River as Life Story

- River begins as small stream in mountains (birth)
- Gathers momentum, meanders over terrain (life journey)
- Enters large body of water (end of life)
- "A river is like a long story. The story unfolds and changes from moment to moment."

### Water = Occupation (Western OT Addition)

Western OTs added a meaning: water represents occupation/activity. They use activity focused on rocks, river walls, or driftwood to "punch a bigger hole through the blockage."

### Strength-Based: Follow the Water

> "Occupational therapists are always looking for the areas of the river that are blue or show even a trickle of water. Why? Because wherever there's a trickle or flow of water, there's the potential for greater flow."

Hornelore implication: Kawa questioning should look for where life was flowing well, not only where it was blocked.

## The 6 Clinical Steps (Interview Flow)

1. **Appreciating the client in context** — "How are things going in general?"
2. **Clarifying the context** — Social/physical environment, relationships, living situation
3. **Prioritizing issues** — Client's perspective on what matters most (rock sizes)
4. **Assessing focal points** — Where can OT intervention help? Where is water flowing?
5. **Intervention** — Activities targeted at rocks, walls, or driftwood
6. **Evaluation** — Use the model again to see if things changed

Hornelore mapping: Steps 1-3 map to Kawa reflection interview mode. Steps 4-6 are clinical and outside Hornelore's memoir scope, but the assessment language ("What did your river look like six months ago? How would you like it to look?") is useful for memoir framing.

## Example Questioning Patterns (from Iwama)

**Life flow:**
- "How are things going in general for you every day?"
- "How has [event] affected you and the activities you do on a day to day basis?"

**Environment (river walls):**
- "Tell me about your family."
- "Tell me about your social connections and the people that you normally interact with on a daily basis."
- "What kind of house do you live in? Is that a problem?"

**Problems (rocks):**
- "What kinds of specific problems and difficulties are you encountering every day?"
- "How big are these problems for you?"

**Personal factors (driftwood):**
- "What are you really good at?"
- "What kinds of things do you really enjoy doing?"
- "What other kinds of things would other people tell you that you're really good at?"

**Temporal comparison:**
- "Let's take a look at another section of your river from six months ago. What did your river look like before any of these problems happened?"
- "How would you like your river to look six weeks from now, six months from now, six years from now?"

## Model Origins

- Developed in mid-1990s in Okinawa, Japan
- Focus group of therapists, students, professors, clients
- Modified grounded theory methodology (adapted for Japanese hierarchical culture)
- Took ~1 year to develop framework
- Born from recognition that Western OT models (MOHO, CMOP) didn't translate to Japanese cultural context
- Key insight: self and environment are inseparable in Japanese cosmology

## Open Source

Iwama explicitly states the model is open for adaptation:

> "If there's a way to be able to modify it in different ways, you have my permission. It is like an open source software."

He asks only for acknowledgment of its OT roots.

## Iwama's Background (relevant to Hornelore narrator parallels)

- Born/raised in Okinawa, Japan
- Family moved to Vancouver, Canada (parents feared Vietnam draft for three sons)
- Switched from physiotherapy to OT after observing OTs at clinical placement
- Started one of first standalone private OT practices in Canada
- PhD in sociology (Japan), further study in health/medical anthropology (Leiden, Netherlands)
- Became Dean of Health and Rehabilitation Sciences at MGH Institute, Boston
- Self-describes as "servant leader"
