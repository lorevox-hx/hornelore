# Parent-session readiness — manual walk script

**Audience:** Melanie (or any thoughtful adult tester, not a developer).
**Date:** 2026-05-06.
**Purpose:** Prove Lori is ready for a real session with Janice or Kent.
**Total time:** ~30–40 minutes split across **two sittings** with a break in the middle.

---

## How this works

You'll sit down with Lori twice. The first time is a real conversation — be yourself, share real stories. The second time is the same conversation continuing after you've stepped away and come back. The whole point is to find out whether Lori still remembers you when you return, whether she handles surprises gracefully, and whether the conversation actually feels like a conversation.

You don't need to memorize anything. Just keep this script open in another window and follow the numbered steps. Type what's in **bold quotes** (or speak it if mic is on). After each step there's a small **OBSERVE** line — that's what to watch for. **NOTE** anything that feels off, weird, or wrong. There's a debrief form at the end.

You're not testing Lori's perfection. You're testing whether sitting with her feels like a respectful conversation, and whether she remembers you when you come back.

---

## Before you start

1. Open the operator tab.
2. Confirm session style is set to **Warm Storytelling** (or **Clear & Direct** if you prefer shorter back-and-forth).
3. Toggle **Cognitive support mode** ON — this gives you the slower pacing parent narrators will get.
4. Click **Start Narrator Session** (or switch to your narrator card if you've used one before).

If you've never been a narrator on this stack, Lori will introduce herself and ask for your name, date of birth, and where you were born. Answer truthfully — your real birthday matters for the age math test later.

---

## PHASE A — Initial sitting (~20 minutes)

### Step 1 — Identity intake

If Lori asks for your name, type your real name (e.g., **"Melanie [Lastname]"**).
If Lori asks for your date of birth, type it as **"M/D YYYY"** (e.g., **"7/15 1972"**) — use a slash and a space, no commas. This format exercises a recent fix.
If Lori asks where you were born, type **"<Your city>, <state>"**.

**OBSERVE:** Does Lori acknowledge each answer warmly without re-asking for it? Does she move forward smoothly?
**NOTE:** Any moment where she re-asks for something you already gave, or sounds like she's reading a form.

### Step 2 — A real moment from earliest years

Click **"Earliest Years"** on the Life Map (the timeline panel showing 7 eras).
Wait for Lori's question.
Answer with one or two real sentences from your actual childhood — a place, a person, a small memory. Don't perform; just say what comes to mind.

**OBSERVE:** Did Lori's question name the era ("during your earliest years…")? Was it warm, single, and complete (one question, not a list)? Did her follow-up after your answer reflect what you actually said?
**NOTE:** If her question felt generic, or if she seemed to ignore the specific thing you mentioned.

### Step 3 — Walk forward through eras

Click **"Early School Years"**. Answer briefly. Click **"Adolescence"**. Answer briefly.
Then click **"Coming of Age"** and answer with something specific — a person, a place, a turning point.

**OBSERVE:** Are her questions varied, or does she repeat the same shape three times? Does she pick up on names you mention?
**NOTE:** Any era where her question felt like a fragment ("The conversations you had then?" — not a real question) or a repeat of something she already asked.

### Step 4 — Stress probe: ask about the era itself

Type **"what are the building years"** (no question mark needed).

**OBSERVE:** Does Lori give a clear, warm explanation of what the era means — roughly a sentence about ages 30s through 50s, career, family? Or does she dodge, or treat it as you wanting to talk about your own building years?
**NOTE:** If her answer was vague, off-topic, or felt like she didn't understand the question.

### Step 5 — Stress probe: a correction

Tell Lori something — anything — about your family or work that's slightly OFF (e.g., **"I have three brothers"** even if you have two; **"I worked at the post office for 20 years"** even if it was 15). Wait for her response.
Then immediately correct it: **"Actually, only two brothers"** or **"Actually, it was 15 years, not 20"**.

**OBSERVE:** Does Lori acknowledge the correction explicitly ("Two brothers — got it")? Or does she keep going as if you hadn't corrected anything?
**NOTE:** If the corrected number/fact appears in her next reply, OR if she ignored the correction.

### Step 6 — Stress probe: ask for your age

Type or say: **"How old do you think I am?"**

**OBSERVE:** Does Lori state your exact age in years (calculated from your DOB), in one short warm sentence? Or does she dodge ("Is there something else on your mind?") or hedge ("you're probably in your 50s")?
**NOTE:** Whether the number is right.

### Step 7 — Stress probe: a vague or refused answer

Click **"Building Years"** and let Lori ask her question. Answer with **"I don't really remember much from then."**

**OBSERVE:** Does Lori accept the gap warmly and offer something different (a different angle, a different era), or does she push for more on the topic you just declined?
**NOTE:** If she pushed when you said you didn't remember.

### Step 8 — Recall test (mid-session)

Type or say: **"What do you know about me?"**

**OBSERVE:** Lori should produce a structured readback — your name, DOB, birthplace, and any stories you've shared so far. Not a wall of text, but a clear summary.
**NOTE:** Anything wrong (wrong birthday, wrong name spelling, mixed-up details). Anything missing (a thing you definitely told her).

📸 **Take a screenshot of this readback.** Save it as `phase_a_recall.png` somewhere you can find later.

### Step 9 — Long pause

Sit silent for **2 minutes.** Don't type. Don't click. Just look at the screen.

**OBSERVE:** Around 2 minutes (since your last input), does Lori gently reach out — a soft sentence on screen like "Take your time, I'm here" or similar? Does it feel calming or pushy?
**NOTE:** Did it appear at all? At what timing did it land?

### Step 10 — Wrap

Type **"I think that's enough for today, I'd like to take a break."**
Click **Wrap Up Session** in the operator panel.

**OBSERVE:** Does Lori say goodbye warmly without trying to push for one more thing?

---

## BREAK

Walk away from the computer for **at least 5 minutes**, ideally 30+. Make tea, take a walk, read your phone. The longer the break, the more useful Phase B is.

When you come back: don't restart anything on the technical side. Just open the same browser tab where Lori is, OR refresh it (Ctrl+R / Cmd+R). The point is to land in the resume state a real returning narrator would land in.

---

## PHASE B — Return sitting (~10 minutes)

### Step 11 — Lori's first words after return

When you return to the tab and the narrator is reopened, Lori should produce a greeting.

**OBSERVE:** Is her first message:
- **(a)** A welcome back? ("Welcome back, Melanie. Where would you like to continue today?" or similar)
- **(b)** A continuation paraphrase? ("Welcome back, Melanie. Last time we were in your earliest years and you mentioned…")
- **(c)** A cold-start? ("Hi Melanie, I'm Lori. I'm here to help you capture your life story…")

**NOTE:** Which of (a)/(b)/(c) you got. (a) is acceptable; (b) is the goal; (c) is broken — Lori forgot you're returning.

📸 **Screenshot the greeting.** Save as `phase_b_greeting.png`.

### Step 12 — Recall test (post-break)

Type **"What do you know about me?"** again.

**OBSERVE:** Does the readback include the same identity (name, DOB, birthplace) you gave in Phase A? Does it include any of the stories or details you shared?
**NOTE:** Anything Phase A captured that Phase B forgot. Anything Phase B includes that Phase A didn't (cross-narrator contamination — should NOT happen).

📸 **Screenshot the readback.** Save as `phase_b_recall.png`.

### Step 13 — Age, again

Type **"How old am I?"** again.

**OBSERVE:** Same answer as Phase A? Different? Wrong?
**NOTE:** Any drift in the number.

### Step 14 — A new story in a new era

Click an era you haven't used yet — **"Later Years"** or **"Today"**. Answer with one or two real sentences.

**OBSERVE:** Does Lori weave in anything from Phase A naturally? Or does she treat it like a brand-new conversation?
**NOTE:** Specifically, does she recall that you're a returning narrator (continuity in tone), or does she sound like she just met you?

### Step 15 — Casual factual question

Type **"What day is it?"**

**OBSERVE:** Does Lori give the actual date? Does she give a wrong date? Does she dodge?
**NOTE:** Exact date she said.

### Step 16 — Wrap

Type **"That's all for today."**
Click **Wrap Up Session.**
